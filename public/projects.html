News Aggregation & User Platform

I built this because I wanted a news experience that wasn't filtered through a single outlet's perspective. The idea was simple: pull from a wide range of global news sources, categorize articles into storyline threads, and let users explore topics without the bias that comes from relying on any one source. On top of that, I added user accounts, a real-time chat feature, and a dashboard, turning it into a full platform rather than just a feed.

My Role: I was the only developer, which meant I owned everything: the database design, user authentication, the news aggregator running in the background, the WebSocket chat, and the frontend. Every decision about how the pieces fit together was mine to make.

Challenges: Security ended up being the hardest part, and honestly the most important. Getting authentication right (password hashing, session tokens, protecting routes) took real research.

Skills & Takeaways: I came away with practical experience in security, real-time systems, and API design, but the bigger takeaway was learning to think about a system as a whole, not just its individual parts. Understanding how the aggregator, the database, the auth layer, and the UI all depend on each other.

Web Development Experiments

An ongoing series of small-scale web applications built to deepen understanding of full-stack developmen covering system architecture, backend/frontend interaction, REST APIs, and database integration. Projects have ranged from simple data-driven UIs to multi-page applications with server-side logic.

Role: Sole developer on each experiment, responsible for design decisions, implementation, testing, and iteration.

Challenges: The biggest challenge was resisting the urge to over-build. Early experiments became bloated because I added features before validating the core idea. Learning to build the minimum viable version first and only extend after it worked was a discipline I had to deliberately develop.

Skills & Takeaways: Gained working knowledge of JavaScript, Node.js, and database design, along with a systems-level understanding of how data flows through a full-stack application. The ability to reason about that flow end-to-end is directly applicable to IT consulting, where evaluating and recommending technology solutions requires understanding what happens under the hood.

LIS 4376 - AI Applications

A semester-long course exploring applied AI spanning machine learning model evaluation, natural language processing, AI ethics, and real-world deployment considerations. Assignments moved from conceptual understanding to hands-on analysis of how AI systems behave in practice.

Role: Student and individual contributor, completed projects independently, analyzing AI outputs, comparing model behavior, and proposing responsible implementation strategies for organizational use cases.

Challenges: Bridging theory and application was the core difficulty. Understanding how a model or package works at a conceptual level is very different from critically evaluating its outputs in a real scenario. Building that critical eye questioning results rather than accepting them required deliberate practice throughout the course.

Skills & Takeaways: Developed the ability to evaluate AI tools through both a technical and ethical lens. I can now assess where AI creates genuine value versus where it introduces bias, risk, or unnecessary complexity.

LIS 4381 - Mobile Web Development

A course focused on building responsive, mobile-first web applications covering viewport design, touch-friendly UI patterns, progressive enhancement, and cross-device testing. Projects ranged from single-page layouts to multi-page apps that had to perform well across phones, tablets, and desktops.

Role: Developer and designer, responsible for both visual design and underlying implementation, including responsive CSS, JavaScript behavior, and accessibility. Making every decision myself reinforced how tightly design and code are linked.

Challenges: Designing for multiple screen sizes simultaneously was harder than expected. A layout that looks right on a desktop can completely fall apart on a phone screen, and fixing one often breaks the other. Learning to use CSS grid and flexbox fluently not just mechanically was the challenge.

Skills & Takeaways: Gained fluency in responsive design, CSS grid/flexbox, and mobile UX principles. I also developed a user-first perspective: small screens force you to prioritize ruthlessly and cut anything that doesn't earn its place. That discipline stripping problems down to what actually matters is a transferrable skill I apply across technical and non-technical work.
